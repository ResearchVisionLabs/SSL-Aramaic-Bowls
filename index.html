Beyond Labels: Visual Invariance in Self-Supervised Learning for Aramaic Bowls

Introduction

Aramaic incantation bowls from Late Antiquity present a highly challenging visual domain characterized by severe material degradation, irregular layouts, and ambiguous letter morphology. This research facilitates the spatial-cultural mapping of these historical artifacts, capturing diverse ritual practices and regional writing conventions without relying on super-resolution methodologies. Instead, we systematically evaluate self-supervised learning (SSL) frameworks by treating augmentation strategies as explicit hypotheses about visual identity.

Dataset Overview

The curated dataset is derived from authentic historical materials, presenting conditions of extreme visual variability.

  • Source Material: 32 Aramaic incantation bowls dated to Late Antiquity (4th-7th centuries CE).
  • Volume: Approximately 8,600 extracted letter-like units (averaging ~260 units per bowl).
  • Annotations: Includes baseline annotations, letter bounding box detections, and segmentation masks.

Experiment Visuals & Augmentation Hypotheses

We formulate three distinct hypotheses regarding visual invariance during self-supervised training:

H1: Appearance Invariance

Assumes identity remains stable under photometric and surface-level changes.

H2: Structural Invariance

Assumes identity is preserved despite geometric and stroke-level variations.

H3: Historical Variability

Assumes identity persists under historical degradation (ink erosion, decay, partial occlusion).

Figure 3: Examples comparing the visual transformations under appearance (H1), structural (H2), and historical variability (H3) assumptions.

Key Results

Evaluation using K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) and Linear Probing demonstrates that augmentation design heavily influences representation quality. Structural augmentation (H2) and simulated historical degradation (H3) consistently outperform appearance-only enhancement (H1) across models.
